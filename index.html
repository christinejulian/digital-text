Text Object Requirements – Playable Titanic Narrative Game

1. Project overview

This document defines the requirements for a playable, story-driven game based on the 1912 sinking of the RMS Titanic. The game centers on passengers and crew who face a sudden, catastrophic disaster, the chaos of evacuation, and the human decisions made under extreme pressure.

2. Historical basis and ethical framing

  • Real event: The narrative is based on the real 1912 sinking of the RMS Titanic in the North Atlantic.
  • Respectful tone: The game must acknowledge that this is a real tragedy where many people died and avoid sensationalizing suffering.
  • Fictional POV: The player controls fictional characters, but the ship, timeline, and major events remain historically grounded.

3. Core themes and sensory focus

“The story of the ship is really good because it has a lot of sensory things in it. Like the cold, the darkness, the panic, heroism and how vulnerable people are.”

  • Vulnerability: Show how fragile human life is against nature and structural failure.
  • Bravery and sacrifice: Highlight difficult moral choices and acts of courage.
  • Class and inequality: Represent different experiences across social classes, with moments of solidarity.
  • Human connection: Focus on relationships formed or tested during the crisis.
  • Sensory atmosphere: Use cold, darkness, sound, and silence to build tension and mood.

4. Player role and goals

  • Role: The player is a fictional passenger (e.g., third-class) who can move between multiple decks and encounter different classes and crew.
  • Main goal: Survive the sinking while deciding who to help and which routes to take.
  • Secondary goals: Help specific NPCs reach lifeboats, deliver messages, or unlock safer paths.
  • Win/lose states: Survival and rescue = success; death or entrapment = failure, with variations based on who was helped.

5. Core gameplay loop

  • Explore: Move through corridors, staircases, and decks; discover routes and blocked paths.
  • Observe: Notice environmental changes (tilt, water, crowd density, lighting) and listen to NPC conversations.
  • Decide: Choose dialogue options, routes, and actions under time pressure.
  • Act: Assist NPCs, open/close doors, clear obstacles, or give up/claim lifeboat seats.
  • Feedback: NPC reactions, access to new areas, and changing danger levels (flooding, lifeboats leaving) respond to player choices.

6. Playable setting and level structure

  • Primary space: The RMS Titanic as a contained, explorable environment.
  • Key playable areas: Third-class corridors, grand staircase, first-class promenade, lifeboat deck, and a service corridor connecting decks.
  • Dynamic levels: Areas become inaccessible over time due to flooding, locked gates, or launched lifeboats, forcing new routes.

7. Narrative phases

  • Phase 1 – Normalcy (tutorial): Short introduction to movement, interaction, and basic dialogue while the ship appears safe and luxurious.
  • Phase 2 – Impact and confusion: After the iceberg strike, subtle changes (vibration, tilt, cold air) appear; information is incomplete and conflicting.
  • Phase 3 – Evacuation gameplay: Timed lifeboat launches, crowding, blocked paths, and moral choices define the main playable segment.
  • Phase 4 – Resolution: Epilogue summarizing the player’s fate and the consequences of their choices.

8. Interaction, controls, and UI

  • Movement: Keyboard/controller movement (WASD/analog stick) to walk/run and navigate the ship.
  • Interaction: Single key/button (e.g., E or A) to talk, inspect, open doors, and assist NPCs.
  • Dialogue: Simple choice-based dialogue with 2–3 options at key moments that affect trust, access, and outcomes.
  • Minimal HUD: Use diegetic cues (shouted orders, lights, water level) instead of heavy UI; small prompts for interactable objects only.
  • Timers: Subtle countdowns or audio cues for time-limited decisions (e.g., lifeboat about to launch).

9. Tone and tragedy representation

  • No gore focus: Avoid graphic depictions of death; emphasize emotional and situational weight.
  • Commemoration: Final screen acknowledges the real disaster and lives lost, with a brief historical note.
  • Serious tone: No comedic or trivializing elements that undermine the gravity of the event.

10. Visual and audio direction

  • Visual arc: Start with warm, bright interiors that gradually shift to colder, darker palettes as danger increases.
  • Ocean and night: Use the surrounding darkness of the North Atlantic to create isolation and vulnerability.
  • Sound design: Ambient ship noise, distant music, crowd sounds, shouted orders, creaking metal, rushing water, and eventual silence.

11. Learning and reflection goals

  • Historical awareness: Encourage curiosity about the real Titanic, its design, and the investigations that followed.
  • Empathy: Help players imagine the emotional reality of passengers and crew facing an unexpected disaster.
  • Systems thinking: Show how class, limited lifeboats, and structural decisions shaped survival chances.

12. Scope for this project

  • Playable prototype: A 10–15 minute vertical slice from shortly after the collision to the launch of several lifeboats.
  • Key decision scenes: At least two scripted moments where the player must choose between self-preservation and helping others.
  • Functional loop: Movement, interaction, dialogue choices, and at least one branching outcome (different endings or mid-level consequences).
  • Design reflection: A short written note explaining how the game remains respectful to the real tragedy and its themes.
